Researchers from Cambridge University and the Czech Academy of Sciences are exploring whether the Arctic Ocean floor could serve as a carbon vault — not through engineered storage facilities, but by deliberately sinking timber from boreal forests into cold, oxygen-poor depths where decomposition effectively stops.

The proposal builds on observations of natural driftwood behavior. Boreal forests stretching from Alaska through Siberia already send timber into Arctic waters through river systems, where it eventually sinks and persists. In a study published in *npj Climate Action*, Ulf Büntgen's team examined wood samples preserved in cold, low-oxygen environments for up to 8,000 years, finding no significant cellulose degradation. Their conclusion: the Arctic seabed could potentially store billions of tons of carbon from harvested boreal timber, particularly from regions where economic value is low and wildfire risk is high.

The scale required is staggering — estimates suggest removing over 10 billion tons of CO₂ annually throughout this century to address anthropogenic warming. Wood vaulting, whether underground or underwater, demands intervention at landscape scale. What's striking here isn't just the technical audacity but the governance vacuum: who decides which forests get harvested for ocean burial? How does this interact with Indigenous land rights across the boreal region? The proposal treats the Arctic seabed as available storage space, a framing that bypasses questions about marine ecosystems and transboundary responsibility.

This is planetary-scale terraforming proposed through peer-reviewed papers rather than public deliberation — a reminder that governance systems for such interventions remain largely theoretical while the technical proposals advance. The cold Arctic depths may preserve carbon for millennia, but the political and ethical architecture for deciding whether to fill them doesn't yet exist.
