When corporate farming swept Asia during the Green Revolution, thousands of locally-adapted rice varieties vanished, replaced by genetically uniform strains vulnerable to single-point failures. Dr Debal Deb has spent three decades reversing that loss — cultivating 1,460 indigenous varieties on Basudha Farm in India, each meticulously maintained to preserve distinct genotypes year after year.

The diversity matters operationally: some varieties withstand floods, others seawater or drought; one contains twenty times the iron of genetically modified 'fortified' strains. Deb tests each rigorously in his Calcutta laboratory, documenting medicinal properties and climate resilience encoded by millennia of farmer innovation. The work functions as a governance intervention — restoring what he calls Vrīhi (Sanskrit for rice) to local farmers, reclaiming seed sovereignty from corporate control.

A new documentary by The Gaia Foundation follows journalist Dan Saladino's encounter with Deb, tracing both the science and the practice. The film treats the seed bank as more than conservation — it's a working alternative to centralized agricultural governance, tested in fields rather than policy documents. Deb hopes the knowledge "will germinate in the minds of the people, as well as in the fields." The archive now stands unparalleled globally, a functioning model of decentralized resilience infrastructure.

The broader pattern is familiar: systems designed for efficiency collapse under novel stress; diversity becomes the precondition for adaptation. Deb's work suggests that governing food systems — like governing ecosystems — requires maintaining variance, not eliminating it. The seeds matter; so does the practice of keeping them alive.
