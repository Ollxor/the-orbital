Mark Carney's Davos admission — that the world is reorganising around great-power competition outside UN rules — named what many have felt: the multilateral order is fracturing, and those racing to please one bloc or another are playing a losing game. The enforcement of international law has always relied less on coercive power than on moral leverage and political will. Both are eroding fast. Slashed aid budgets, weakened economies, and the hollowing credibility over Gaza and US aggressions have diminished Western leadership, while China, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE expand influence with a vision of multilateralism stripped of meaningful scrutiny.

But the UN human rights system still has one structural advantage: the one-country-one-vote principle. Raphaël Viana David of the International Service for Human Rights argues that small and medium states — particularly from the Global South — hold real power if they choose to wield it collectively. Recent signs are encouraging: Albania, the Netherlands, Chile, Kyrgyzstan, and Kenya rallied 90 countries last week at the Human Rights Council in a joint pledge to defend human rights multilateralism. Small island states like Mauritius and the Maldives are joining Latin American nations to resist Chinese and Russian efforts to defund UN human rights work in budget negotiations.

This isn't nostalgia for a golden age that never was. Human rights progress has always depended on truth-seeking, public pressure, and committed movements — not great-power benevolence. The question now is whether states are willing to invest political courage and resources in a system that still offers tangible impact: assisting governments on AI policy, climate adaptation, fiscal justice, and investigating atrocities the Security Council ignores. Governance, as The Garden has long held, is not merely a structure to admire but a practice to sustain. At the UN, strength in numbers remains a viable strategy — if enough actors recognise the cost of abandoning it exceeds the cost of showing up.
