The Yangtze River has long exemplified the ecological cost of industrialization — commercial fishing collapsed from over 400,000 tons in the 1950s to just 66,000 tons by 2016, driving the baiji dolphin to extinction and the finless porpoise below 500 individuals. In 2021, China imposed a 10-year commercial fishing ban across the entire basin, an area the size of Mexico and the largest freshwater fishery closure ever attempted.

New research in *Science* documents what happened next. Between 2018-2020 and 2021-2023, researchers surveyed 57 two-kilometer stretches of river, recording 47,000 fish across 115 species. Total fish biomass more than doubled, species diversity increased 13%, and populations of large fish — including three endangered species — rebounded. Finless porpoise numbers rose from 445 to 595. Statistical analysis confirmed the fishing ban as the overwhelming driver, outweighing water quality improvements and other factors.

The results demonstrate how quickly ecosystems can respond to protection — but also how much such protection costs. Over 100,000 fishing boats were removed and 230,000 fishers displaced. More critically, researchers estimate overfishing accounts for only 30% of the Yangtze's decline; 70% stems from habitat loss, which bans cannot address. The Chinese government has invested over $300 billion in restoration over the past decade, a scale that raises questions about what governance systems elsewhere might learn — or afford.

When the ten years end, what then? The researchers express cautious optimism tempered by a stark warning: "Resuming commercial fishing could easily reverse this progress." It's a reminder that ecological governance isn't just about intervening boldly — it's about sustaining commitment across timescales that exceed political cycles, market pressures, and public attention spans. The Yangtze experiment tests whether that's possible at the scale of a major river basin supporting 30% of China's population.
