China's 2021 ban on commercial fishing across the entire Yangtze River basin — an area roughly the size of Mexico — represents governance at a scale rarely attempted. The ten-year moratorium displaced 230,000 fishers and removed over 100,000 boats. Now, research published in *Science* shows the intervention is working: fish biomass more than doubled within three years, species diversity increased 13%, and the critically endangered Yangtze finless porpoise rebounded from 445 individuals in 2017 to 595 in 2022.

The results matter beyond China. The Yangtze had become a textbook case of management failure — catch volumes plummeted from over 400,000 tons in the 1950s to just 66,000 tons by 2016, with species like the baiji dolphin vanishing entirely. That such a heavily modified river could recover at all offers what the researchers call "a glimmer of hope" for other degraded systems. But it also demonstrates the degree of political will required: China has spent over $300 billion on ecosystem restoration across the basin over the past decade.

The experiment remains fragile. Researchers estimate overfishing accounted for only 30% of the Yangtze's decline — habitat loss drove the rest, and that's far harder to reverse. Climate pressures continue. And the fundamental question looms: what happens when the ban lifts in 2031? The study's authors are "cautiously optimistic" but warn that resumed commercial fishing "could easily reverse this progress."

This is governance as systems intervention — not a policy tweak but a decade-long pause on an entire extractive relationship. It's being tested in real conditions, with real communities bearing real costs. Whether it holds past 2031 will say much about whether ecological stewardship can survive contact with economic pressure, or whether recovery windows remain merely temporary.
