Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index 2025, released this month, marks a troubling milestone: the global average score has fallen to 42 out of 100, the lowest level in over a decade. Of 182 countries and territories assessed, 122 score below 50 — what the organization terms "serious corruption problems." Only five countries now score above 80, down from 12 a decade ago. The index draws on 13 independent data sources and uses a scale where 0 signals "highly corrupt" and 100 "very clean."

The data reveals a pattern that governance researchers have long suspected but rarely see quantified at this scale: corruption thrives where civic space contracts. In nearly two-thirds of countries whose scores declined significantly since 2012, Transparency International documents "a worrying pattern of restriction on freedoms of expression, association and assembly." Thirty-six of the 50 countries with major declines also saw civic space shrink. Countries rated as "full democracies" average a CPI score of 71; "flawed democracies" sit at 47; non-democratic regimes at 32.

The human cost is stark. Since 2012, 829 journalists have been murdered in non-conflict zones worldwide, including 150 killed while investigating corruption — five of them in 2025 alone. Over 90% of these killings occurred in countries scoring below 50. The report calls for governments to strengthen justice systems, ensure independent oversight, make political finance transparent, and protect civic space and media freedom.

There are modest signs of institutional response. In December, governments meeting in Doha agreed on measures to strengthen implementation of the UN Convention against Corruption, with civil society organizations pushing for stronger peer review mechanisms. The UK and Norway recently published anti-corruption strategies committing to discussions on creating an International Anti-Corruption Court — a proposed tribunal to prosecute "grand corruption" that now has backing from eight countries. Democracy Without Borders endorses the effort. The court would function as a form of transnational accountability infrastructure, though whether it can address the underlying civic space crisis that the CPI data reveals remains an open question.
