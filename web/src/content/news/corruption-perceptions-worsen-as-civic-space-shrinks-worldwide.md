The global average score on Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index has fallen to 42 out of 100 — the lowest level in more than a decade. The 2025 index, published in February and drawing on 13 independent data sources across 182 countries, reveals that 122 territories now score below 50, indicating what the organization calls "serious corruption problems in most parts of the planet." Only five countries score above 80, down from 12 a decade ago.

The most striking pattern in the data links deteriorating corruption perceptions directly to shrinking civic space. In nearly two-thirds of countries whose scores have significantly declined since 2012, Transparency International documents a "worrying pattern of restriction on freedoms of expression, association and assembly." The correlation is stark: countries with more open civic space consistently score lower on corruption, while those with restricted freedoms see average scores steadily decline. This isn't abstract — 829 journalists have been murdered in non-conflict zones since 2012, including 150 covering corruption stories.

The report arrives as some governments begin testing new accountability mechanisms. The UK and Norway recently published anti-corruption strategies committing to discussions on creating an International Anti-Corruption Court to prosecute grand corruption. Eight countries now back this effort, while civil society organizations push for stronger international peer review under the UN Convention against Corruption. These moves suggest a growing recognition that corruption isn't merely a local governance failure — it's a planetary systems problem requiring transnational institutional innovation.

Whether these institutional experiments can reverse the trend remains uncertain. Transparency International calls for strengthened justice systems, independent oversight of public spending, transparent political finance, and protected civic space. The underlying logic echoes The Garden's thesis: governance systems cannot function in isolation from the democratic practices and civic freedoms that allow them to be tested, challenged, and refined. Corruption flourishes where feedback loops break down — where citizens cannot see, speak, or organize.
