Democracy Without Borders has released a brochure sketching their vision for reimagining global cooperation at a moment when multilateral institutions face simultaneous legitimacy and capacity crises. The publication surveys concrete institutional reforms — including proposals for a UN Parliamentary Assembly, transnational citizens' initiatives, and revised Security Council structures — that aim to make international governance more representative and responsive.

The brochure reflects a growing recognition that effective planetary stewardship requires not just better policy coordination but fundamental redesign of how decisions get made across borders. Democracy Without Borders frames these proposals as infrastructure for democratic participation at scale, addressing the governance gap between nation-state sovereignty and problems that ignore borders entirely.

What distinguishes this approach from abstract calls for global solidarity is its attention to institutional mechanics — the actual mechanisms through which citizens might exercise voice in transnational decisions. Whether these specific proposals gain traction matters less than the underlying question they raise: what institutional forms allow democratic practice to operate at the scale of contemporary challenges? The brochure functions as both advocacy document and design inventory, cataloging options for governance practitioners willing to work beyond inherited structures.
