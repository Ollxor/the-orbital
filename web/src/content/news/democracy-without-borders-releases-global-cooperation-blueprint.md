Democracy Without Borders has published a brochure outlining its vision for restructuring global cooperation frameworks. The document arrives as multilateral institutions face compounding legitimacy crises — from climate finance deadlocks to the erosion of treaty-based governance architectures.

The brochure positions itself as both diagnosis and design brief, arguing that existing UN structures remain locked in mid-20th-century assumptions about sovereignty, representation, and decision-making authority. It sketches alternatives: weighted voting mechanisms that reflect population and climate vulnerability, parliamentary assemblies with direct electoral legitimacy, and governance experiments that move beyond the nation-state as the sole unit of representation.

What distinguishes this from typical policy advocacy is its emphasis on institutional form as political substance — the recognition that how we cooperate shapes what becomes possible. The document functions less as a manifesto than as an invitation to treat global governance as an active design challenge, not a fixed inheritance. Whether these ideas gain traction will depend on their capacity to move from paper to practice, from proposal to prototype.
