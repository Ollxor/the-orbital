The European Space Agency gathered researchers in early February to advance what they call "digital twins" of Earth — continuously updated computational models that simulate forests, ice sheets, rivers, and increasingly, the human systems entangled with them. Unlike static Earth observation datasets or conventional climate models, these twins ingest real-time satellite data to test what-if scenarios: flood risk across Italian watersheds given four days of accumulated rainfall, agricultural stress patterns under shifting weather, carbon trajectories shaped by fire suppression or logging policy.

The distinction matters for governance. As Freya Muir of Future Earth noted at the workshop, digital twins integrate "human activity, economics, and policy changes into the simulations" — they're designed not just to forecast, but to stress-test interventions before they're deployed. Projects like DTE Hydrology and EOAgriTwin already offer dashboards for national governments and local authorities testing responses to water insecurity or disaster mitigation. The European Commission's Destination Earth initiative aims to knit these component twins into an interconnected operational system.

The technology faces familiar tensions. Machine learning shortcuts that make planetary-scale computation feasible also propagate errors and uncertainties baked into gap-filled satellite records. And as adoption expands, the infrastructure demands — sustainable data centers, equitable access for researchers in the Global South — become governance questions themselves. Future Earth is funding projects in Delhi and Ethiopia to build decision support systems using free satellite data, an effort to ensure these tools serve not just those with computational wealth but communities facing the sharpest climate risks.

What emerges is a new substrate for democratic experimentation: not merely observing Earth systems, but rehearsing responses to their unraveling. The question is whether these twins will remain technical marvels or become instruments of collective foresight — tested, contested, and shaped by those whose futures they simulate.
