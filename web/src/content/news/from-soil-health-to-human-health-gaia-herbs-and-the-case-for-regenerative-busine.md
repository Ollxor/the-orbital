The herbal supplement industry has grown from $5 billion to over $60 billion in three decades, yet Gaia Herbs operates on a different premise: that the quality of medicine is inseparable from the health of the land that produces it. Kyle Bliffert, the company's CEO, oversees a nearly 270-acre Regenerative Organic Certified farm in the Blue Ridge Mountains of North Carolina — one of the few operations of its scale attempting to prove that ecological restoration and commercial viability can coexist.

Bliffert's 33-year trajectory through the supplement industry traces a broader shift in how wellness is understood. Early functional medicine practitioners treated supplements as medical tools; now the question has moved upstream to farming systems, soil biology, and what regeneration actually means when applied to commercial agriculture. When Hurricane Helene flooded the farm in October 2024, followed by a factory fire in January 2025 that halted production for 87 days, the operation faced a stress test not unlike the climate shocks that will increasingly challenge food and medicine supply chains globally.

The company's emphasis on Regenerative Organic Certification — a standard that combines soil health, animal welfare, and farmworker fairness — suggests an evolving governance model for the supplement industry. Whether these practices can move from boutique to baseline remains an open question, but Bliffert's assertion that herbs are "having their moment" points to a potential decade-long window where consumer demand might actually reward the harder, slower work of regenerative systems. The test isn't just whether ashwagandha supports stress response, but whether markets can support the farms that grow it without extracting from the ecosystems they depend on.
