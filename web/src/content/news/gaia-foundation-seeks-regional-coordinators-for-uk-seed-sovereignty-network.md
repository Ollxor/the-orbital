The Gaia Foundation is expanding its UK Seed Sovereignty Programme with two regionally-based coordinator roles covering southern and western England. The positions reflect a practical challenge in ecological governance: how to scale networked, place-based stewardship without losing the local knowledge that makes it work.

The roles are explicitly structured for practitioners who want to remain grounded — 21 hours per week, home-based, designed for growers who keep their hands in the soil while coordinating regional network development. This matters because seed sovereignty isn't an abstract policy position. It's a practiced alternative to consolidated seed systems, requiring both agronomic skill and the ability to weave connections across communities, bioregions, and international movements.

Gaia has worked at the intersection of seed diversity, climate resilience, and Indigenous knowledge systems for nearly four decades, supporting communities in Africa to revive local seed diversity by restoring traditional governance alongside agrobiodiversity. The UK programme, nearly a decade old now, extends that logic: governance of food systems begins with who controls the genetic material, and how that knowledge circulates.

The coordinators will support training programmes, multi-stakeholder partnerships, and grassroots network development — the practical infrastructure of a food system organized around different principles than the one currently dominant. Applications close soon and are being handled through CharityJob.
