The Global Alliance for the Rights of Nature is recruiting interns for two-to-six-month placements across four streams: communications, design and web development, tribunal coordination, and organizing. Based in Quito but accepting remote participants worldwide, the positions offer structured entry into a network advancing legal personhood for ecosystems — a governance innovation that reframes nature as rights-holder rather than property.

The tribunal stream is particularly notable. GARN's International Rights of Nature Tribunal operates as both legal proceeding and public pedagogy, documenting cases where extractive systems collide with ecological integrity. Interns will support research, documentation, and communications for sessions that have previously addressed deep-sea mining, deforestation, and water privatization. The work is both archival and anticipatory: building the jurisprudence for a legal paradigm still under construction.

Applications close January 20. The positions are unpaid, with in-person participants covering their own expenses in Ecuador — a structural constraint that narrows accessibility, though the remote option broadens geographic reach. For those entering governance work, the opportunity represents rare proximity to movement infrastructure translating ecological ethics into enforceable frameworks.

What distinguishes this from standard environmental internships is the focus on governance as transformation rather than advocacy. GARN coordinates alliances across regions, supports Indigenous communities asserting territorial rights through nature-centered law, and produces resources for municipalities and activists implementing Rights of Nature locally. The internships position participants not as observers but as contributors to a living experiment in how societies encode their relationship to the non-human world.
