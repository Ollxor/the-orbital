From Nepal to Tanzania, 2025 saw youth-led uprisings erupt in over eleven countries, targeting economic inequality and corruption with shared symbols — including manga icons from *One Piece* — that traveled from the Philippines to Kenya to Serbia. The protests often met brutal state response: Iran reported 30,000 killed since December, Tanzania 700 on election day alone, Kenya at least 31 during anti-government marches. What these movements share is fury at rigged economies — 68 percent of global respondents in IPSOS's 2025 report agreed the system favors elite wealth — and what they lack is a unified political destination.

Gen-Z's mobilization doesn't point clearly toward democracy. Young voters are fueling populist surges in Europe, with ultra-right gains in Germany and France, even as the same cohort shows the highest support for global governance innovations like a world parliament — net approval of 19 percentage points for ages 18-35, compared to 0.5 for older groups. In Bangladesh, a student-led revolution delivered a contested BNP victory in February; in Nepal, coalition parties court youth votes ahead of March elections. The pattern suggests not ideological coherence but systems strain — governance structures struggling to metabolize demands that originate in material insecurity but branch in multiple directions.

This is governance testing itself in real time, not through policy debates but street-level confrontation. As Democracy Without Borders notes, the question isn't whether young people can mobilize — they clearly can, at mortal cost — but whether political architectures can flex enough to hold their demands. Economic precarity explains a third of recent populist gains, per recent research, which means the same conditions fueling protests also feed authoritarian drift. What remains certain: the elite status quo, as the article puts it, is reaching its boiling point. What emerges afterward depends on whether existing systems prove adaptable or merely brittle.
