Between 2025 and early 2026, youth-led uprisings erupted in over eleven countries — from Nepal and Madagascar to Iran and Tanzania — all voicing the same core grievances: economic inequality, corruption, and democratic backsliding. Gen-Z protesters adopted shared symbols across borders, including skull-and-crossbone imagery from the manga *One Piece*, first used in the Philippines to signal resistance against injustice. But the unity of their aesthetic belied a more complex reality. While these movements represent the largest coordinated youth mobilization in decades, they don't point toward a single ideological future — and many regimes have met peaceful demonstrations with lethal force. At least 30,000 were killed in Iran since December, 700 in Tanzania during October elections, and dozens more in Kenya, Nepal, and Peru.

The uprisings stem from shared material conditions: 68 percent of global respondents in the 2025 IPSOS Populism Report feel the economy is rigged for elites, and economic insecurity explains roughly one-third of recent populist surges worldwide. Yet Gen-Z's political direction remains ambiguous. In Europe, young voters are fueling gains by ultra-right parties in Germany and France. In Bangladesh, students who led a 2024 revolution contested the Bangladeshi Nationalist Party's February victory. Meanwhile, the same demographic shows the strongest support — 19 percentage points net positive — for establishing a world parliament, according to recent Democracy Without Borders polling.

This is governance at a breaking point: systems designed for older equilibriums now face a generation testing their limits in the streets. The question isn't whether young people can organize — their capacity for coordination is proven. It's whether existing political architectures can absorb demands for equity and accountability without collapsing into either authoritarian suppression or populist capture. As Nepal heads to elections in March and interim governments navigate power vacuums, the outcomes will reveal whether protest energy can translate into institutional transformation — or whether it dissipates into fragmented ideological channels. What's certain is that the elite status quo, as Democracy Without Borders notes, is reaching its boiling point. How governance systems respond will shape the next decade of planetary politics.
