The 12th Plenary of the Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services convened in Manchester from February 3-9, where 150+ member governments approved a fast-tracked assessment on business and biodiversity. The numbers tell a blunt story: in 2023, public and private financial flows harming nature totaled $7.3 trillion, while just $220 billion was directed toward biodiversity conservation and restoration. The gap isn't just economic — it's a governance failure at the intersection of markets, accountability, and ecological limits.

Developed over three years by 79 experts from 35 countries, the report integrates perspectives from science, private sector actors, and Indigenous Peoples and local communities — a recognition that biodiversity governance must operate across epistemic boundaries. Dr. David Obura, IPBES Chair and Earth Commission member, framed the assessment as urgent input for meeting Global Biodiversity Framework targets, particularly Target 15 on business transformation. The report underscores what The Garden often observes: governance frameworks mean little without mechanisms to make ecological dependency and impact legible, accountable, and actionable.

The assessment concludes that businesses are pivotal to reversing biodiversity loss, yet insufficient information about their impacts, dependencies, and risks continues to hinder progress. This isn't merely a data problem — it's a question of governance architecture. Without systems that surface ecological consequences in real time and link them to decision-making processes, transformation remains rhetorical. The IPBES plenary functions as a form of intergovernmental deliberation, but the harder work lies in translating assessments into governance experiments that businesses, governments, and communities can actually test and refine.

Future Earth's delegation attended the plenary and will share reflections soon — an opportunity to observe how science-policy platforms navigate the gap between synthesis and implementation, between knowing what must change and building the structures that enable it.
