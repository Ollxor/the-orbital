The Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services completed its first fast-track assessment last week in Manchester, where 150 member governments approved findings that reframe business activity as a central governance challenge for planetary systems. The numbers tell the story bluntly: $7.3 trillion in public and private flows harming nature in 2023, against just $220 billion directed toward conservation and restoration.

Developed by 79 experts from 35 countries, the assessment integrates scientific, private sector, and Indigenous perspectives — a methodological choice that acknowledges no single knowledge system can map the full terrain of how economies depend on and destabilize living systems. Dr. David Obura, IPBES Chair and Earth Commission member, positioned the report as urgent infrastructure for meeting the Global Biodiversity Framework's Target 15, which addresses business impacts directly.

What makes this assessment notable is less its conclusions — that businesses are "pivotal" to reversing biodiversity loss — than its frank acknowledgment that insufficient information about dependencies and risks continues to obstruct meaningful action. It's a governance problem, in other words: the tools for measuring, regulating, and redirecting economic activity remain mismatched to the scale of ecological transformation underway. The report arrives midway through a decade that will determine whether global frameworks like the Paris Agreement function as binding commitments or aspirational artifacts.
