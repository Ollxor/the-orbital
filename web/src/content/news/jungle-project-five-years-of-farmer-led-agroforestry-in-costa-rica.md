Five years into a fiscal sponsorship partnership, Kiss the Ground and Costa Rica's Jungle Project offer a case study in how back-office infrastructure can enable grassroots ecological work to scale. What began with graduates from a soil advocacy training program has become a network of 22 regenerative breadfruit farms across Limón and Pejivalle, with over 18,600 plants — breadfruit, citrus, cacao, avocado — established across 20 hectares.

The model is farmer-first: local technicians and agroforestry specialists provided 43 training sessions and organized field trips to institutions like EARTH University, building both technical capacity and inter-generational knowledge transfer. This isn't just planting — it's governance at the farm level, where decisions about soil, biodiversity, and food security are made in practice, not in policy documents.

Jungle Foods, the project's commercial arm, has launched breadfruit flours, noodles, specialty cacao, and coffee into values-aligned supply chains, proving that regenerative production can compete in markets structured around extraction. Funding from Alter Eco, Erol Foundation, Trees That Feed, and others sustained the work; in 2025, CATIE selected Jungle Project for non-reimbursable capital through a business incubation initiative — a signal that regenerative agriculture is moving from margin to method.

Fiscal sponsorship, as Kiss the Ground frames it, is solidarity infrastructure: the administrative scaffolding that lets practitioners focus on land rather than paperwork. The partnership shows what becomes possible when institutions align behind farmer-led stewardship — not as beneficiaries of intervention, but as architects of ecological and economic resilience.
