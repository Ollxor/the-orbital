A €40,000 loan from Rewilding Europe Capital will enable Marco Ferraz, founder of nature tourism company Ambieduca, to renovate a traditional house in Vilar de Amargo, a village in Portugal's Greater Côa Valley. The investment — part of a growing portfolio supporting nature-positive enterprises — will expand accommodation options in a region where rewilding has become the economic engine. Ferraz, who leads wildlife tours and cultural heritage experiences, will convert the property into "Casas de Villar-Rewilding Spot," housing up to four guests and potentially creating another local job. This isn't venture capital chasing returns; it's patient finance designed to demonstrate that ecological recovery can anchor rural livelihoods.

The Greater Côa Valley offers a useful case study in governance through aligned incentives. Since 2021, the Wild Côa Network has linked nearly 60 local businesses — guides, producers, hospitality providers — around a shared bet on nature comeback. Rewilding Portugal coordinates the network; Rewilding Europe Capital provides capital; Wilder Places connects customers. The model treats landscape restoration not as preservation but as productive infrastructure, with measurable effects on employment, visitor flows, and community participation. Ferraz's expansion addresses a specific gap: northern valley villages lack accommodation, limiting visitor distribution and economic diffusion.

What makes this relevant beyond tourism is the governance architecture it implies. The loan isn't a grant; it expects repayment, creating accountability loops between ecological outcomes and business performance. The network structure distributes coordination without centralizing control. And the model scales through replication, not extraction — three Wild Côa businesses have now received capital, with each success strengthening the case for nature-positive investment elsewhere. This is governance as patient systems-building: aligning financial instruments, business networks, and ecological processes in ways that make restoration economically legible.

Rewilding Europe's model — tested across multiple European landscapes — suggests that nature recovery can function as economic policy when supported by appropriate financial and organizational infrastructure. The Côa Valley experiment offers early evidence that such systems can work at the scale of villages and small enterprises, where governance is most directly felt. Whether this approach can inform larger-scale land management remains an open question, but the underlying insight is clear: ecological restoration generates economic opportunity when paired with patient capital and coordination mechanisms that help local actors capture value from landscape change.
