The Human Rights Foundation has launched a governance classification tool that departs from the numerical scoring systems that dominate democracy measurement. The Tyranny Tracker instead uses qualitative thresholds across 45 indicators — grouped under electoral competition, freedom of dissent, and institutional accountability — to distinguish democracies, hybrid authoritarian regimes, and full autocracies. The approach reflects a harder question: not how democratic a country scores, but whether its institutions retain the capacity to constrain power and enable genuine political competition.

Of 179 territories assessed, 74 are classified as democracies, 25 as hybrid authoritarian, and 80 as fully authoritarian. The index currently covers 162 UN member states, finding that three-quarters of the global population lives under some form of authoritarian governance. India, Indonesia, Pakistan, and the Philippines are classified as hybrid regimes — electoral systems persist, but courts and legal mechanisms systematically tilt outcomes against opposition victories. China, Russia, Nigeria, Ethiopia, and Vietnam are ranked as fully authoritarian, where nonviolent democratic transition remains theoretical.

The methodology centers on what researchers call "executive-led erosion" and judicial capacity as decisive signals. In a companion essay for the Journal of Democracy, HRF researchers argue existing indices "struggle to capture the moment when democracies break down," often treating Thailand and Singapore as flawed democracies when their judiciaries function as tools of entrenchment rather than constraint. By contrast, Mexico — despite mounting executive pressure — retains courts capable of blocking initiatives, while Ukraine's wartime governance is distinguished from authoritarian consolidation by the persistence of contestation and oversight.

The tracker will be reassessed annually, with earlier updates for major shifts. It's a diagnostic instrument, not a verdict — but one that foregrounds the institutional substrates that make accountability possible. Governance, the framework implies, is less about abstract freedoms than the lived capacity of institutions to check power when it overreaches.
