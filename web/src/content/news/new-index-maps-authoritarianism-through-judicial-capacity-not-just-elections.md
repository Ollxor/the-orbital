The Human Rights Foundation has launched a governance classification tool that diverges from conventional democracy indices by focusing on qualitative thresholds rather than aggregated scores. The Tyranny Tracker covers 179 countries and territories, classifying 74 as democracies, 25 as hybrid authoritarian regimes, and 80 as fully authoritarian — a framework that places 75 percent of the world's population under some form of authoritarian rule.

What distinguishes the tracker is its attention to executive-led erosion and judicial capacity as litmus tests for regime type. Writing in the Journal of Democracy, HRF researchers argue that existing indices struggle to capture the moment democracies break down, often misclassifying hybrid authoritarian states like Thailand and Singapore as "flawed democracies" despite courts and legal mechanisms systematically tilting the field against genuine competition. The framework uses 45 indicators grouped under electoral competition, freedom of dissent, and institutional accountability — assessing whether these features are present, weak, or absent.

The approach yields counterintuitive classifications: Mexico and Ukraine remain democracies under pressure, the researchers argue, because judicial systems retain capacity to constrain executive power, even amid drug cartel violence in one case and full-scale war in the other. The distinction matters for governance systems worldwide — it suggests that the presence of elections tells us less than the integrity of institutions that mediate power between votes. The tracker will be reassessed annually, with adjustments when major events warrant.

This is not merely taxonomic exercise. For those working on democratic innovation and planetary governance, the emphasis on felt institutional capacity — on whether courts actually check executive overreach, whether dissent can organize without state retaliation — points toward governance as lived experience rather than constitutional text. The tracker becomes a map not just of where democracy exists, but of where the infrastructure for collective self-correction remains intact.
