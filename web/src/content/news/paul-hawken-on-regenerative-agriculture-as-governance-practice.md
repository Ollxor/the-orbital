Paul Hawken has spent decades tracing how systems of extraction — colonial, industrial, agricultural — share a common logic: objectify the living world, then manage it to death. In a wide-ranging conversation with Kiss the Ground's Evan Harrison, he frames regenerative agriculture not as climate tech or farming innovation, but as a correction to 500 years of "seizing and selling people, cultures, homelands, forests, minerals, and land to the highest bidder."

The distinction matters. Hawken, author of *Regeneration* and *Carbon: The Book of Life* and founder of Project Drawdown, argues that most climate solutions still treat nature as object — "putting lipstick on the pig of consumption" rather than addressing the extractive economy upstream. Regenerative agriculture, by contrast, begins with relationship: soil as mother, not medium; food systems as living communities, not supply chains. "When you create more life in the soil," he notes, "you get healthy plants, people, and animals."

This isn't metaphor. Hawken founded Erewhon in the 1960s after discovering he'd unknowingly sold customers products labeled organic that weren't — a revelation that led him to stock only what he could verify by visiting farms directly. Those farmers, he realized in hindsight, were practicing regenerative methods decades before the term existed, never having "gone over to the chemical side." The practice preceded the policy framework, a pattern familiar to governance innovators: sometimes the doing must come first, the theory later.

Hawken closes with the image of fire-triggered succession — wildflower seeds dormant for a century, germinating only after intense heat. "The world is on fire literally, ecologically, and socially," he observes. "At the same time, there is this fire-triggered succession. Movements are arising worldwide to protect our children, forests, cultures, land, and seas." Regenerative agriculture, in this frame, becomes one expression of a broader emergence: governance practices rooted in stewardship rather than seizure, testing what it means to relate to living systems as kin.
