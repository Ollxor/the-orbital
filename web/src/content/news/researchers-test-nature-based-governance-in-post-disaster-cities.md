Researchers from The University of Tokyo, Stockholm Resilience Centre, and the University of Lodz gathered in Tokyo in February to launch a three-year study on nature-based infrastructure as a governance instrument in disaster recovery. The project — part of Future Earth's Urban Knowledge-Action Network — focuses on post-disaster sites in Japan's coastal Tohoku region and aims to inform reconstruction planning in Ukrainian cities facing compounding crises of conflict, climate, and demographic decline.

The research treats green infrastructure not as ornamental but as load-bearing civic architecture. Field visits to Shinchi and neighboring municipalities in Fukushima and Miyagi examined reconstructed coastal parks with evacuation functions, hybrid sea defenses integrating ecological and engineered systems, and compact public housing designed for aging populations. These sites offer concrete instances of how ecological restoration becomes governance practice — shaping not just landscapes but social cohesion, intergenerational memory, and community reintegration after trauma.

The project's five research objectives include analyzing civic ecology practices as pathways for healing, examining green infrastructure design across urban-rural gradients, and developing policy frameworks for nature-positive recovery. Particular attention will be paid to multigenerational perspectives and the reintegration of returnees and veterans in post-conflict settings. The work responds to a broader recognition that urban resilience requires governance systems that are felt and tested in practice — not merely debated in policy documents — and that operate at the intersection of ecological restoration, social cohesion, and institutional design.

By centering case studies from regions experiencing population aging, disaster recovery, and geopolitical instability, the collaboration offers a testing ground for governance approaches that treat nature-based infrastructure as democratic infrastructure — spaces where communities negotiate their relationship to risk, memory, and collective futures.
