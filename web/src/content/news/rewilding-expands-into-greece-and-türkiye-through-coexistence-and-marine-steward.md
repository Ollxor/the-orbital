The European Rewilding Network now includes CALLISTO, a Greek organization working on human-carnivore coexistence, and Marine Rewilding Türkiye, which partners with small-scale fishers to restore coastal ecosystems. The additions bring the network to over 100 member initiatives and extend its geographic reach into two new countries — part of a deliberate strategy to connect practitioners across borders and ecosystems.

CALLISTO, founded in 2004, focuses on brown bears and grey wolves in Greece's mountain landscapes. Its approach centers on preventive infrastructure like electric fencing and waste management, paired with community engagement that treats coexistence as a governance challenge, not merely a technical one. Marine Rewilding Türkiye, led by the Mediterranean Conservation Foundation, embeds marine rangers within fishing cooperatives and builds what it calls "community-based governance" — locally rooted enforcement paired with scientific monitoring of seagrass beds and protected areas.

Both initiatives join the network seeking knowledge exchange rather than top-down coordination. "We saw first-hand the value of working alongside organizations that share a strong commitment to coexistence," says Spyridon Psaroudas, CALLISTO's general coordinator, referencing their collaboration with Rewilding Apennines. Gizem Akdoğan of the Mediterranean Conservation Foundation emphasized learning from "nature-based economies" and sharing methods for scaling restoration with coastal communities.

The network's expansion illustrates a pattern: rewilding increasingly operates as distributed governance infrastructure, linking practitioners who test coexistence models, commons management, and ecosystem restoration in specific places. Knowledge flows horizontally; success is measured in wolf populations, recovered seagrass, and farmers willing to share space with bears — outcomes that emerge from collaboration, not decree.
