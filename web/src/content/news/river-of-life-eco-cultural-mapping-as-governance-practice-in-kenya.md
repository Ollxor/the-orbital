The Tharaka community at the foothills of Mount Kenya has become the first in Africa to complete Life Plans — eco-cultural maps that function as both memory work and governance instrument. The methodology, developed by Indigenous peoples in the Colombian Amazon and shared through the Gaia Foundation's African Earth Jurisprudence Collective, begins with elder dialogues that recall pre-colonial relationships to territory. These conversations generate three maps: one of the ancestral past, one of the colonial present, and one charting a future where "the autonomy, abundance, and health of Tharaka's humans and more-than-humans is restored."

What emerges is not abstract planning but a River of Life — named for the Kithino River — into which flow distinct streams of work: seed revival, rotational grazing systems called Marithia, sacred natural site protection, and the reconstruction of the Gaaru, a traditional meeting place for ecological governance. Elder Salome Gatumi describes the return to Indigenous seeds that "were born in this soil, so they know how to adapt to our climate," while Mbura M'Rugia speaks of shift grazing that reunites divided land and herds, restoring genetic diversity and climate resilience. The process is participatory, intergenerational, and rooted in seasonal calendars that track constellations, moon cycles, and ritual timing.

The governance dimension is tangible. When the Kenyan government proposed the Mutonga Dam — backed by multinational financing — the project would have flooded half of Tharaka, including the sacred Kibuka Falls. It was cancelled. As elder and Earth Jurisprudence practitioner Simon Mitambo reflects, "Today, we thank our ancestors that the dam was cancelled." The Life Plans gave the community not just a map but the collective coherence to assert territorial authority — governance felt and tested, not merely debated.
