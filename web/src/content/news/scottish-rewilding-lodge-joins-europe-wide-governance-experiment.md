Ballintean Mountain Lodge, tucked into Glen Feshie in Scotland's Cairngorms, has spent three decades practicing what most conservation discourse only recently named: process-led rewilding. Now it joins Rewilding Europe's network of over 100 initiatives across nearly 30 countries — a distributed experiment in what happens when land governance shifts from management to trust, from intervention to enabling.

The 52-hectare property, owned by conservation photographer Peter Cairns and his wife Amanda Flanagan, demonstrates rewilding as both ecological practice and knowledge infrastructure. Natural grazing, free-flowing river dynamics, and regenerating native woodland shape the land; meanwhile the lodge hosts land managers, policymakers, and NGOs for immersive training — governance education through embodied encounter with recovering systems. "As rewilding matures, effective engagement with communities, land managers, and policymakers is becoming ever more important," Cairns notes, pointing to the gap between ecological possibility and institutional readiness.

The European Rewilding Network functions as governance laboratory: peer-to-peer learning among practitioners testing how to cede control, reintroduce species (water voles and beavers are next at Ballintean), and design tourism that funds rather than extracts. The lodge now appears on Wilder Places, Rewilding Europe's booking platform — an attempt to scale nature-positive travel as economic model. Whether such networks can reshape land tenure and decision-making beyond their nodes remains open, but the Highlands offer 30 years of evidence that ecosystems, given space and time, govern themselves with more resilience than humans typically manage.
