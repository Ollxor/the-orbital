Cambridge and Czech Academy researchers are proposing something audacious: harvest timber from boreal forests, transport it via rivers, and sink it into the cold, anoxic depths of the Arctic Ocean, where it could sequester carbon for thousands of years. The proposal builds on "wood vaulting" — burying biomass in environments where decomposition stalls — but replaces the need for thousands of purpose-built facilities with what they frame as natural infrastructure.

The numbers make the stakes clear. Climate models suggest we need to remove and sequester over 10 billion tons of CO₂ annually throughout this century to manage anthropogenic warming. The boreal forest — stretching from Alaska through Siberia — stores roughly 1 trillion tons of carbon in trees, deadwood, and peat. Lead researcher Ulf Büntgen and colleagues note that boreal timber has low economic value and sits near major river systems already carrying driftwood into the Arctic. Their earlier work showed that wood in cold, low-oxygen conditions retained cellulose content unchanged over 8,000 years.

But the proposal raises governance questions that matter as much as the carbon math. Who decides whether Siberian or Canadian forests should be logged for sequestration? What constitutes consent from Arctic coastal communities, or from nations with Arctic territorial claims? The researchers acknowledge the Arctic's low biodiversity but frame it as storage space — a utilitarian logic that sidesteps questions of Indigenous sovereignty and marine ecosystem integrity. This isn't just an engineering problem; it's a test of whether planetary-scale interventions can be governed through anything resembling democratic deliberation, or whether climate urgency will justify technocratic fait accompli.

The proposal exemplifies a recurring pattern in climate response: solutions that work on paper but require governance structures we haven't yet built. The Arctic Ocean isn't empty space awaiting optimization — it's a commons with existing claimants, ecological dynamics, and political complexity. Whether wood vaulting happens there, or anywhere, will depend less on carbon accounting than on our capacity to negotiate planetary stewardship across borders, disciplines, and power asymmetries.
