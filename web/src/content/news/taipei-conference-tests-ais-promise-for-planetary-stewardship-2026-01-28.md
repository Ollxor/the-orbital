Can artificial intelligence become a tool for planetary stewardship, or does it merely add another layer of complexity to already fraught governance systems? That tension animated the 2025 International Conference on AI for a Sustainable Society, held November 13-14 at Academia Sinica in Taipei, where researchers and policymakers examined what they called a "dual transformation" — the need to innovate in both technology and sustainability simultaneously.

The conference brought together more than ten sessions exploring AI applications from climate resilience and Nature-based Solutions to green finance and disaster early warning systems. Academician C.-C. Jay Kuo from USC questioned whether "Green AI" is achievable at all, while Dr. Camilo Alejo from Future Earth's Canada Hub demonstrated what he termed "smart synergy" — ways AI might strengthen ecological systems rather than simply monitor them. The opening ceremony itself staged this duality: a chamber ensemble, a DJ, and AI-generated visuals performing together, a deliberate gesture toward hybrid intelligence.

What made ICAISS2025 notable was its insistence on bridging theory and practice. Teams from Salesforce Taiwan, Alpha FinTech, and emerging startups worked alongside academic researchers, testing whether governance frameworks for AI can actually shape how the technology develops — not merely respond to it after deployment. The question of who controls these tools, and for what ends, remained implicit throughout: can societies develop AI that serves ecological and social goals, or will existing power structures simply find new digital instruments?

Taiwan's positioning at this intersection — between advanced AI development and sustainability science — reflects a broader pattern in planetary governance: small states and regions often become laboratories for governance experiments that larger powers debate but rarely test. Whether this conference generates durable frameworks or becomes another venue for aspirational thinking will depend on whether its "continued international collaboration" translates into concrete governance practice.
