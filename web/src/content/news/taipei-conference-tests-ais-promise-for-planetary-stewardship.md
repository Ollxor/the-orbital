Over two November days at Academia Sinica in Taipei, the 2025 International Conference on AI for a Sustainable Society (ICAISS2025) assembled researchers, policymakers, and industry practitioners around a deceptively simple question: can artificial intelligence meaningfully advance sustainability goals without compounding the risks it claims to solve? The gathering of several dozen organizations — from Future Earth's Canada Hub to Salesforce Taiwan and emerging fintech startups — reflected what organizers called a "dual transformation" challenge, requiring simultaneous innovation in both technology development and ecological practice.

The program spanned climate resilience tools, green finance applications, and governance frameworks for algorithmic accountability. Four keynote addresses set the terms: USC's C.-C. Jay Kuo questioned whether "Green AI" is achievable at scale, while Future Earth's Camilo Alejo demonstrated how machine learning might strengthen nature-based solutions when embedded in participatory design processes. The conference's strength lay less in technical demonstrations than in sustained exchange between academic researchers and practitioners testing these tools in live contexts — debris flow early warning systems, circular economy supply chains, ESG investment algorithms.

What emerged was not triumphalism but constructive friction. Sessions on AI governance explored fairness, transparency, and inclusion as design requirements, not afterthoughts. The gathering opened with an unusual artistic gesture: a chamber ensemble, DJ, and AI-generated visuals performing together — a deliberate reminder that technological capacity means little without deliberate choices about how and for whom it operates. Taiwan's positioning at this intersection of AI development and sustainability science offers a useful laboratory, provided the work remains accountable to the communities and ecosystems it claims to serve.

The organizers frame ICAISS2025 as the start of ongoing international collaboration rather than a milestone event. That framing matters. Governance systems capable of stewarding planetary-scale technologies must be practiced and refined, not merely proposed at conferences. Whether this network can sustain that iterative work — bridging research, policy, and lived experience — will determine if it becomes infrastructure or another well-intentioned conversation.
