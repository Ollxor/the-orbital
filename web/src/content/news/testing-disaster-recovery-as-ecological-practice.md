Urban researchers from Tokyo, Stockholm, and Łódź gathered in February to launch a three-year study of nature-based infrastructure in disaster and conflict recovery — using Japanese coastal towns rebuilt after the 2011 tsunami as primary field sites. The project, anchored in Future Earth's Urban Knowledge-Action Network, links The University of Tokyo, the Stockholm Resilience Centre, and the University of Łódź's Social-Ecological Systems Analysis Lab to examine how green infrastructure can serve as recovery architecture in cities facing overlapping crises: climate shocks, biodiversity collapse, demographic decline, and geopolitical instability.

The Tokyo kickoff included a field visit to coastal Tohoku — Shinchi in Fukushima and neighboring municipalities — where participants examined memorial facilities, reconstructed coastal parks with dual evacuation functions, and compact public housing designed for aging populations. These sites offer concrete evidence of how disaster recovery intersects with demographic change and sustainability transitions, particularly in regions experiencing population decline. The project treats these landscapes not as case studies but as governance laboratories where nature-based solutions are already being tested under real conditions.

Research will focus on five objectives: the role of green infrastructure in preparedness and recovery; civic ecology practices as pathways for post-conflict healing; diverse nature-based crisis responses; infrastructure design across urban-rural gradients; and integration into long-term policy. A particular emphasis falls on multigenerational perspectives and the reintegration of returnees and veterans — with findings intended to inform recovery processes in Ukrainian cities. The approach recognizes that resilience is not built through blueprints alone, but through the felt experience of communities rebuilding amid compound uncertainties.

The collaboration reflects a broader shift in how urban governance is researched: not as abstract planning frameworks, but as embodied, place-specific practices that emerge from disaster, adapt to ecological limits, and must account for the social fabric of return and recovery. Whether these insights can translate across contexts — from Japan's aging coastlines to Ukraine's war-affected cities — remains an open question the project is designed to test.
