Mark Carney's Davos speech named what many already felt: the world is reorganising around great-power competition outside multilateral rules. The UN human rights system — chronically underfunded, politically fragile, increasingly targeted by Beijing and Moscow — appears especially vulnerable. Yet a different pattern is emerging at the Human Rights Council, one that tests whether collective governance can still function when unilateral force dominates.

Last week, 90 countries — half from the global south — signed a joint pledge to defend human rights multilateralism. The coalition, convened by Albania, the Netherlands, Chile, Kyrgyzstan and Kenya, commits to strengthening the HRC, securing stable funding for UN rights bodies, and responding to crises based on objective criteria rather than political convenience. It's a modest instrument, but it demonstrates a principle central to governance innovation: that formal equality (one country, one vote) can generate real leverage when smaller actors organise deliberately.

The context matters. China has moved methodically to reshape UN human rights work, lobbying to gut budgets, punish cooperation with investigators, and reframe scrutiny as interference. Its new Group of Friends of Global Governance promises "true multilateralism" while opposing inquiries into atrocities in Xinjiang and elsewhere. The US, meanwhile, holds UN finances hostage even as its own credibility erodes over Gaza and imperial overreach. This isn't a UN crisis — it's a states' crisis, exposing whether governance systems can function when their most powerful members abandon the social contract.

What the 90-country pledge reveals is that multilateral institutions still offer pathways for collective action, even in a fractured landscape. Whether that potential translates into durable practice depends on political courage, sustained funding, and willingness to confront violations everywhere — from Gaza to Xinjiang to Minnesota. Governance systems, as The Garden has argued, must be tested and inhabited, not merely defended in principle. The coalition strategy is one such test, measuring whether strength in numbers can still shape outcomes when power politics dominate.
