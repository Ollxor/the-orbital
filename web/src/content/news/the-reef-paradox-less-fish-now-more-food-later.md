Scientists have quantified what they call a "perverse dilemma" in coral reef management. A new PNAS study modeling 2,000+ reef sites worldwide found that properly managed reefs could sustainably produce enough fish to feed 2.5 million additional people across Indonesia, the Philippines, and Tanzania alone — regions already facing malnutrition. The gap between current catches and sustainable potential amounts to 9,000 meals per square kilometer. But reaching that potential requires reducing fishing pressure for 6 to 70 years, depending on recovery approach and reef condition. Some reefs in Kenya, Mauritius, and Oman now hold less than 10% of their baseline fish biomass.

The research — led by marine scientist Jessica Zamborain-Mason at KAUST — reveals what happens when governance systems can't bridge the temporal gap between present need and future possibility. "To reduce fishing pressure for more fish protein over the long term, there must be ways to offset short-term nutritional deficits," notes ecologist Sean Connolly from the Smithsonian Tropical Research Institute. "That's the biggest challenge." The study doesn't propose specific mechanisms, but gestures toward agricultural alternatives, substitute fisheries, or food imports — none simple for small island nations with limited land.

This is governance-as-patience, a quality rarely available to communities fishing for daily survival. The calculus is straightforward: leave fish in the water now, feed more people later. The practice is anything but. Most overfished reefs lack monitoring infrastructure, scientific capacity, or regulatory systems. More fundamentally, they lack the social safety nets that would make restraint viable. The study makes visible a familiar pattern — ecological recovery demanding sacrifices from those with the least margin to sacrifice, measured against benefits that won't materialize for decades.

What the research doesn't address is perhaps more telling than what it does: who designs the transition, who bears the cost of waiting, and what governance arrangements might make long-term stewardship feel possible rather than punitive. Without answers to those questions, the potential remains precisely that — a number on a model, not food on a table.
