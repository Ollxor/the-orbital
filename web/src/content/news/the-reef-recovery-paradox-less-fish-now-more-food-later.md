Scientists have quantified what might be called the cruelest math in marine governance: coral reef fisheries could sustainably yield 9,000 additional meals per square kilometer if fish stocks were allowed to recover. The research, published in PNAS and based on surveys of over 2,000 reef sites, found that overfished reefs worldwide contain on average just 32% of the fish biomass they could support at maximum sustainable yield — defined as half the biomass found on pristine, unfished reefs. In the most depleted sites in Kenya, Mauritius, and Oman, fish populations have crashed to less than 10% of their potential.

The benefits of recovery would flow disproportionately to communities facing malnutrition: Indonesia could feed 1.4 million additional people with recommended fish intake, the Philippines 800,000, Tanzania over 500,000. But the temporal bargain is severe. Even with complete fishing bans, median recovery time is six years; under gentler management approaches, nearly 50 years. In the hardest-hit areas, restoration could take 70 years — and that's before accounting for habitat loss from coral bleaching.

"To reduce fishing pressure for the sake of more fish-derived protein sustainably over the long run, we need ways to compensate for the nutritional shortfall in the short term," notes ecologist Sean Connolly of the Smithsonian Tropical Research Institute. "That's the biggest challenge." The researchers don't specify what those compensatory measures might be, though they gesture toward alternative agriculture, imported food, or diversified seafood sources — solutions that bump against the hard constraints of small island geography and economic capacity.

This is governance as temporal redistribution: asking communities with minimal slack to bear immediate costs for benefits that may not materialize for generations. It's also a test case for whether planetary stewardship frameworks can move beyond the abstract appeal of long-term thinking to actually supporting the people being asked to wait. The science is clear on what reefs could provide. The governance question — who pays for the gap, and how — remains unresolved.
