Democracy is being contested not just in institutions but in language itself. Research consistently shows that citizens equate democracy primarily with elections and majority rule while neglecting the institutional architecture that sustains it — checks and balances, minority rights, judicial independence. This gap matters: when democracy is reduced to voting alone, illiberal actors can claim legitimacy while hollowing out its substance. And the misunderstanding cuts both ways: citizens in autocracies tend to overestimate how democratic their systems are, while those in established democracies underestimate the quality of their own.

A large-scale experiment across 33 countries with over 40,000 participants tested whether brief animated videos could shift this understanding. The intervention was minimal — a single three-minute video explaining core democratic principles. Yet the results were consistent: increased support for democracy, improved factual knowledge, and reduced acceptance of authoritarian alternatives. The effects appeared across both democracies and autocracies, and were strongest among young people and those least engaged in politics.

The mechanism seems to be accessibility rather than depth. Short videos meet people in the spaces they already inhabit — often dominated by misinformation — and provide clear, relatable explanations without partisan framing. Paid social media campaigns can reach beyond echo chambers to audiences skeptical of democracy itself. Importantly, content focusing on rights, institutions, and accountability had more durable effects than messages emphasizing economic performance, and hopeful narratives outperformed fear-based appeals.

This is not civic education as ritual performance in classrooms, but as scalable infrastructure for democratic literacy. The research suggests that governance resilience depends not just on institutional design but on whether citizens understand what those institutions are meant to do — and that such understanding can be cultivated through surprisingly modest interventions. If democracy must be felt and tested rather than merely debated, it must first be legible.
