Democracy faces a crisis of comprehension. Research consistently shows citizens worldwide reduce democracy to elections and majority rule, missing the liberal architecture — checks and balances, minority rights, judicial independence — that sustains it. This misunderstanding has consequences: people in autocracies believe they already live in democracies, while those in functional systems underestimate what they have. When governance is understood only as voting, illiberal actors can claim democratic legitimacy while dismantling democratic substance.

A new experiment across 33 countries with more than 40,000 participants tested whether short online videos could shift this. Individuals randomly exposed to three-minute animated explanations of democratic principles showed measurable changes: increased support for democracy, improved factual knowledge, reduced acceptance of authoritarian alternatives. The effects held across regime types and were strongest among young people and the politically disengaged — precisely those least reached by traditional civic education.

The mechanics matter. Videos focusing on rights, institutions, and accountability proved more durable than those emphasizing economic performance. Positive narratives outperformed fear-based appeals. And paid social media campaigns reached beyond echo chambers to audiences skeptical of democracy itself. This isn't about replacing embodied democratic practice — town halls, assemblies, participatory budgeting — but recognizing that understanding precedes engagement. If governance systems must be felt and tested, they must first be legible.

The implications are practical. Even small organizations can deploy targeted content at scale, meeting citizens in the information environments they already inhabit. Digital civic education won't save democracy alone, but as an evidence-based, cost-effective complement to institutional reform, it addresses a foundational need: you cannot defend or renew what you do not understand.
