The numbers arrive with unusual consensus. Billionaire wealth reached $18.3 trillion in 2025 — an 81% increase since 2020, according to Oxfam's latest assessment. The G20 committee report notes that between 2000 and 2024, the richest 1% captured wealth gains 2,655 times larger than the bottom 50%. The World Inequality Report 2026 adds that these disparities stem from political choices, not economic inevitability. Three separate analyses, published within months of each other, converge on the same inflection point: extreme wealth concentration is actively undermining democratic accountability.

The governance implications are specific and measurable. Countries with high inequality — defined as a Gini coefficient above 0.4, a threshold now exceeded by 83% of nations — are up to seven times more likely to experience democratic erosion, per research cited by Oxfam. The G20 committee traces the mechanism: concentrated wealth translates to concentrated media ownership, campaign finance power, and institutional distrust. Meanwhile, 2.6 billion people cannot afford a healthy diet, and food insecurity has risen by 335 million since 2019. The distance between these realities is itself a governance failure.

The G20 committee proposed creating an International Panel on Inequality, modeled on the IPCC, to provide governments with independent assessments of trends and policy impacts. The recommendation reflects a recognition that inequality requires the same systematic monitoring as planetary climate systems. Whether governments will establish such a body — and whether it would have teeth — remains an open question. But the diagnosis is no longer in doubt: governance systems built to serve democratic publics are being reshaped by wealth concentration at a pace that outstrips institutional capacity to respond.

This is not abstract theory. It's a question of whether democratic forms can hold when economic power operates at planetary scale while accountability remains fragmented across national jurisdictions. The reports document the problem with precision. What they cannot yet offer is a tested model for governance that remains responsive to publics rather than to concentrated capital. That work — designing, testing, and iterating democratic forms under conditions of extreme inequality — remains the central challenge.
