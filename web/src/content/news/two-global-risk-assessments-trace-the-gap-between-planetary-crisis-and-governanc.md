Two major risk assessments published this month converge on a shared diagnosis: the systems we've built to manage global threats are failing at the moment they're needed most. The Global Challenges Foundation reports that seven of nine planetary boundaries — the thresholds defining humanity's safe operating space — have now been breached, most recently ocean acidification. The World Economic Forum's survey of 1,300 experts finds geoeconomic confrontation and state-based conflict now eclipse climate risks in the two-year outlook, even as environmental dangers remain dominant over the decade ahead.

The GCF report frames the problem as one of institutional mismatch: "Planetary commons cannot be negotiated with," it states, yet governance mechanisms remain weak and fragmented. It calls for a legally binding framework on autonomous weapons and highlights experimental forms of participation — citing partners including Democracy Without Borders and the Iswe Foundation — as examples of the institutional creativity now required. The WEF assessment, bleaker in tone, documents a steep rise in uncertainty: 57% of respondents expect a turbulent or stormy outlook over the next decade, with only 1% anticipating calm. "The future is not a single, fixed path but a range of possible trajectories," the report notes, stopping short of prescription.

What both documents make visible is the erosion of the institutional substrate beneath global cooperation — trust, legitimacy, shared norms. Environmental risks, while severe over the long term, have dropped in short-term salience as geopolitical confrontation and technological instability crowd the frame. The WEF notes that "adverse outcomes of AI" jumped from 30th to fifth place in the ten-year ranking, reflecting how rapidly new risks are compounding older ones. This is governance under conditions of radical uncertainty — not a policy puzzle to be solved, but a condition to be navigated through forms of collective practice we're still learning to build.
