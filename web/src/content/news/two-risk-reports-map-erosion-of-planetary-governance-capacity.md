Two major risk assessments released this month map a widening gap between planetary threat and institutional capacity. The Global Challenges Foundation reports that seven of nine planetary boundaries have now been breached, most recently ocean acidification, while the World Economic Forum's survey of 1,300 experts finds geoeconomic confrontation overtaking climate as the most immediate perceived crisis. Both reports frame the challenge as fundamentally institutional: existing governance mechanisms cannot match the speed or scale of interconnected risks.

The GCF assessment is particularly pointed about what it calls "systemic Earth risks" — climate tipping points, biodiversity collapse, weapons of mass destruction, AI in military decision-making, and asteroid impact. Its language is blunt: "Planetary commons cannot be negotiated with." The report calls for legally binding frameworks on autonomous weapons and cites citizen participation mechanisms — including work by Democracy Without Borders and the Iswe Foundation — as examples of institutional creativity worth scaling.

The WEF outlook is dominated by uncertainty. Half of respondents expect a "turbulent or stormy" next two years, rising to 57% over the coming decade. Only 1% foresee calm. The report's framing is telling: "a multipolar or fragmented order in which middle and great powers contest, set, and enforce regional rules and norms" — multipolarity without multilateralism. Environmental risks, while still severe in long-term projections, have dropped in short-term salience, displaced by misinformation, AI governance failures, and state conflict.

Neither report offers detailed solutions, but both underscore a shared diagnosis: governance systems built for a different era are breaking under compound pressure. The question is not whether transformation is needed, but whether it can emerge before the boundaries collapse entirely.
