The numbers trace a governance crisis in formation. Global billionaire wealth now stands at $18.3 trillion — an 81% real-terms increase since 2020, according to Oxfam's latest assessment. Over the same period, billionaires multiplied their fortunes 2,655 times faster than the bottom 50% of the global population. This isn't wealth creation; it's wealth extraction on a scale that reshapes the political landscape itself.

Three concurrent reports — from Oxfam, a G20-mandated expert committee, and the World Inequality Report 2026 — converge on a shared diagnosis: extreme concentration undermines the institutional foundations that make democratic governance possible. Countries with high inequality are up to seven times more likely to experience democratic erosion, Oxfam finds, as concentrated wealth translates directly into political power through media ownership and campaign finance. The G20 committee warns that 83% of countries, representing 90% of the world's population, now operate with income inequality (Gini above 0.4) that actively fuels polarization and institutional distrust.

The committee's recommendation — an International Panel on Inequality modeled on the IPCC — signals recognition that inequality requires the same governance infrastructure as climate change: independent assessment, comparative data, and policy evaluation that operates beyond national political capture. The parallel is apt. Both challenges require not just technical solutions but transformed systems of accountability.

Meanwhile, 2.6 billion people cannot afford a healthy diet, and food insecurity has risen by 335 million since 2019. The World Inequality Report emphasizes what the data makes plain: these outcomes reflect political and institutional choices, not economic inevitability. Which means governance systems that currently produce them can be redesigned to produce something else.
