Jon A. Juárez won last year's Rewilding Europe Award with a photograph of an Atlantic sturgeon drifting into Sweden's Göta River — the culmination of days documenting a team transporting fish from Germany, building acclimation cages, and coordinating a multi-year reintroduction effort. The image works, he says, because it distills all that invisible labor into a single frame. "I saw not just a photograph, but a promise: that ecosystems can recover when long-term commitment and passion, collaboration, and science come together."

Now serving as a judge for this year's competition (deadline 1 March), Juárez describes what he looks for: "Rewilding is as much about people reconnecting with landscapes as it is about wildlife returning, and I'm drawn to images that capture that relationship — coexistence, resilience, and real impact." His own work — spanning aerial, underwater, and terrestrial perspectives — reflects a conviction that conservation photography isn't just public storytelling. Strong visuals, he notes, also play crucial roles behind the scenes: in grant applications, policy discussions, conference presentations. In a competitive research landscape, compelling imagery can make restoration projects more accessible and help them stand out.

Juárez's approach parallels The Garden's thesis that governance systems must be felt and tested, not merely debated. Rewilding initiatives are governance performances in their own right — collaborative experiments in land management, species stewardship, and long-term ecological commitment. The photographs that document them make visible the human infrastructure of restoration: the teams that build cages, the biologists who tag fish, the communities that negotiate coexistence. These images don't just celebrate wildlife's return; they reveal the patient, unglamorous work of rebuilding relationships between people and place.

He's currently working as staff photographer at the Leibniz Institute for Zoo and Wildlife Research, documenting projects including GAIA Initiative's environmental early warning system and WildtierNah Berlin, a government-funded program coordinating care for injured urban wildlife. The images accumulate — bats, lions, sturgeon, horses returning to the Kazakh steppe — each one a record of governance happening at the species level, one release at a time.
