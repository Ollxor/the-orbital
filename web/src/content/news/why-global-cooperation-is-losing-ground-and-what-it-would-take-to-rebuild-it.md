A new survey of 22,000 people across 31 countries reveals a troubling pattern: support for international cooperation is declining across generations, income levels, and geographies. Between 2024 and 2025, every measure of global solidarity — from willingness to fund global problems to support for enforcement powers in international institutions — fell. Even younger generations, long assumed to be more globally minded, now show no more internationalist sentiment than their grandparents.

The findings, published in the 2025 Global Solidarity Report by Democracy Without Borders and Ipsos, point to a deeper crisis than simple nationalism. When people stop believing that cooperation delivers tangible results — pandemic response that works, climate action that protects livelihoods, financial systems that reduce inequality — they withdraw support from collective institutions at every scale. The mechanisms are familiar to anyone studying democratic decline: eroding trust, rising polarization, and a vocal minority actively opposing cooperation as a threat rather than a tool.

What makes this more than another poll about attitudes is the connection to governance capacity itself. International institutions like the WHO face political abandonment not because they're inherently flawed, but because they lack the anchoring in public expectation that domestic institutions enjoy. No politician would survive shutting down public hospitals, yet walking away from the WHO registers as legitimate policy. The difference isn't function — it's felt ownership.

The report suggests one pathway forward: visible delivery on issues that matter. Inequality — particularly the explosion of financialized wealth that crosses borders as easily as viruses — may be the test case. A UN tax convention is now formally underway, and Brazil's G20 leadership secured a landmark declaration on international tax cooperation in 2025. These are not fantasies but live experiments in whether multilateral governance can restore faith in collective problem-solving. The question isn't whether people support global cooperation in principle. It's whether they'll ever see it work in practice.
